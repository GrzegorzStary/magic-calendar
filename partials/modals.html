These traditions eventually influenced the creation of Santa Claus and Father Christmas as we know them today.

The Declaration aims to establish a universal standard of living that every individual is entitled to, encouraging all UN member states to strive towards this ideal. Bhutan National Day

Bhutan National Day, celebrated every year on 17 December, is a vibrant occasion that commemorates the coronation of the first king of Bhutan, Ugyen Wangchuck, in 1907. This significant holiday transforms the Changlimithang Stadium into a hub of festivities, where Bhutanese people gather to honour their rich history and the establishment of the monarchy that continues to shape their nation today.

History of Bhutan National Day

The roots of Bhutan National Day trace back to the early 17th century when the country was unified under the guidance of Buddhist monk Ngawang Namgyal. However, it was Ugyen Wangchuck, the Ponlop of Tongsa, who emerged as a pivotal figure in the late 19th century, uniting Bhutan and establishing a monarchy after abolishing the previous dual system of governance. His coronation on 17 December 1907 marked the beginning of the Wangchuck dynasty, which still reigns today as a symbol of stability and unity for the Bhutanese people.

How to Observe Bhutan National Day
  • Shut Down Your Office: Join in on this nationwide celebration by taking a break from work and immersing yourself in the festivities.
  • Prepare to Welcome Guests: The day is filled with preparations across various venues, including the Palace of the Druk and Changlimithang Ground, where everyone comes together to celebrate.
  • Visit Changlimithang Ground: Experience the excitement firsthand by attending the celebrations at this iconic venue, where dignitaries and locals gather for a day of joy.
Fascinating Facts About Bhutan National Day
  1. Commemorates a Historic Coronation: The day serves as a reminder of Ugyen Wangchuck's bold steps towards unifying Bhutan.
  2. Celebrated Annually Since 1907: This national holiday has been observed for over a century, marking 114 celebrations to date.
  3. International Attendance: Delegates from around the world come to join in on this momentous occasion.
  4. Changlimithang Ground's Legacy: This stadium has been a central site for National Day celebrations since its construction in 1974.
  5. Processions Featuring Ugyen Wangchuck's Statue: His statue is carried during processions, symbolising his lasting legacy as Bhutan's first Druk Gyalpo.

Bhutan National Day is not just a public holiday; it’s a heartfelt celebration that brings together communities to reflect on their shared history and look forward to their future as one nation under the Dragon King.

International Migrants Day

International Migrants Day, observed annually on 18 December, shines a spotlight on the protection of migrants' human rights and celebrates their invaluable contributions to societies worldwide.

Since time immemorial, humans have been on the move, seeking greener pastures for food, water, and shelter. Sometimes, conflict forced people to leave their homelands in search of new beginnings. As borders were drawn and nations formed, migrants often played a pivotal role in shaping new countries. Take the United States, for instance, established by English migrants seeking religious freedom. Between 1900 and 1915, a whopping 15 million migrants made their way to American shores from various corners of the globe.

Notable Migrations Throughout History
  • 1944: 700,000 people fled Chechnya for Central Asia to escape Joseph Stalin's regime.
  • 1947-1950: Over 15 million people moved between India and Pakistan due to religious conflicts.
  • 1948-1950: 2 million Chinese migrants left China for Taiwan and other countries to escape communism.
  • 1882-present: 3.6 million Jews have returned to their homeland, Israel, from all over the world.

While migrants often leave their familiar surroundings in search of a better life, they frequently face a host of challenges in their new homes. These can include language barriers, poverty, employment difficulties, discrimination, inadequate housing, and transportation issues. Access to economic assistance and healthcare can also prove tricky.

How to Celebrate International Migrants Day
  • Ponder how your country might be different without migrants
  • Explore your family's migration history
  • Chat with someone who has migrated to learn about their experiences
  • Watch enlightening documentaries about migration, such as '4.1 Miles' or 'Human Flow'
  • Learn about famous migrants who've made significant contributions to their adopted countries
  • Spread awareness on social media using #MigrantsDay

The United Nations General Assembly proclaimed 18 December as International Migrants Day on 4 December 2000, recognising the growing number of migrants worldwide. The date marks the adoption of the International Convention on the Protection of the Rights of All Migrant Workers and Members of their Families in 1990.

So, let's raise a cuppa to the diverse tapestry of humanity and the incredible journeys that have shaped our world!

Discover the fascinating history of migration, the challenges faced by those seeking a better life, and how you can make a difference in their lives!
